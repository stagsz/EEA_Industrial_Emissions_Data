Management Summary 
 
Throughout the world, there are approximately 2,869 active thermal waste treatment plants by the end of 2023. 
Not all of them are generating energy and can therefore not be classified as Waste -to-Energy plants (WtE 
plants).  
The Asian share of the overall capacities is 7 6% and therefore still smaller than the share of the number of 
plants with 7 8%. The reason for that lies in the small average size of Japanese plants, which amounts to 
around 60,000 annual tonnes. The average size of a European waste incineration plant is more than three 
times larger and amounts to about 200,000 annual tonnes.  
 
Plants worldwide 
 
 
Considering a poor utilisation of less than 50% in Japan, China by far combusts or gasifies the largest amounts 
of waste worldwide (based on the analysis of tonnes). 
The thermal waste treatment plants in the USA are even larger and have an average capacity of around 
400,000 tonnes of waste per year. The main reasons for this development are the energetic use of the plants 
and the higher pressure in terms of costs. 
The different technical structures in the submarkets become especially obvious when not only considering the 
incineration capacities in tonnes but also the calorific value of the waste. Due to the larger organic share in 
their waste, Asia usually has a considerably lower calorific value than Europe or North America. Some  plants

Treatment capacities by mass and energy 
 
 
In terms of incineration technology, grate combustion is dominating the market. It is not only preferred for 
untreated waste but in many cases also for processed waste, such as refuse-derived fuel from MBT plants. 
In terms of age, the incineration lines have been online for an average of about 20 years. Waste incineration 
plants are young where the markets have boomed in the past years, like in the Asian countries or the UK.  
The thermal waste treatment business will continue to grow at a global level. We expect the number of thermal 
waste treatment plants to grow from 2,869 at the end of 2023 to about 3,104 by 20331.  
We furthermore expect the global treatment capacities to increase from around 5 76 million annual tonnes at 
the end of 2023 to about 726 million annual tonnes within the same period. This means that the international 
portfolio will increase by about 2.7% annually. 
Even though efforts to increase the recycling of waste have increased, the main reasons for the global growth 
of thermal waste treatment remain: 
• The waste disposal pressure continues to increase, especially in economically growing countries due

Projection of capacity growth by region 
 
Europe’s share in the global WtE market will decline further – even though European capacities are still 
growing. The main reason for this is that new awardings in the UK, Europe’s formerly booming WtE market, 
have practically come to an end in 2023. We still forecast demand for new thermal treatment capacity in 
countries that do not yet fulfil the EU’s waste legislation, e.g. in Poland. Even in saturated markets such as 
Germany, Italy and France, some new projects are appearing. 
We expect an additional demand for WtE capacities in Europe beyond the forecast period, driven by the 
Circular Economy Package of the EU.

1 This year’s highlights and trends 
 
In this first chapter, we will highlight some recent developments  on the Waste -to-Energy market. These are 
described in more detail in the following chapters. 
In the years to come, the global WtE market will continue to be led by Asia. China will remain the largest WtE 
market, but this growth will decline in the years to come. 
CO2 emissions and their pricing remain one of the most important issues in the WtE industry. However, 
implementation remains difficult. 
 
Chinese WtE market experiencing a major downturn 
The Chinese Waste-to-Energy market is experiencing a major downturn. Over the recent years already, new 
awardings have declined considerably. However, since 2023, this decline has even accelerated.  
The country’s populated eastern coastal provinces are already facing overcapacities and forced plant 
shutdowns. Therefore, in the future, we expect new commissionings to rather focus on the central and western 
regions. Moreover, in the medium and in the long term, greater recycling efforts will also lead to a decreasing 
demand for additional WtE plants. However, despite these developments, China will continue to lead the global 
market development, albeit at a significantly lower level compared to the previous years. China will still account 
for nearly half of all capacities worldwide to be built from 2024 to 2033 – in regard to tonnes per year. 
In the years to come, the global Waste -to-Energy market will continue to be led by the Asian countries. 
However, other promising markets, such as Thailand, Indonesia, India, and Malaysia, are not yet able to 
compensate for the declining Chinese market. In most of these countries, sufficient and sustained financial 
and regulatory support from the government continues to be lacking. In addition, many of these countries are 
struggling with political instability. 
In general, the European market will remain stable in the years to come. The long-lasting boom in new project 
awardings in the UK has come to an end . However, we still see market potential in countries such as Poland 
and the Czech Republic, as well as in markets like Italy and France.  
 
Discussions about carbon management 
At present, the potential of implementing carbon capture and storage (CCS) and carbon capture and utilisation 
(CCU) technology is widely being discussed in the WtE sector. CCS and CCU describes the technology used 
for the capture of CO2 at the emitter and the subsequent permanent storage or utilisation of the emissions.  
In recent years, several carbon capture projects have been announced at WtE plants in several countries, 
especially in Europe. The currently most promising CCS and CCU projects are located in the North Sea, and 
projects at geographically adjacent WtE facilities to utilise the storage sites are underway. A first CCU system 
is already operational at a WtE plant in the Netherlands, where the captured CO 2 is used to supply local 
greenhouses.

imports decrease and WtE capacities are closed, the heat supply must be replaced.

Management summary 
India experiences a WtE boom, mainly driven by waste disposal problems, new waste management handling 
rules, and governmental incentives. However, most projects still continue to fail, amongst others, due to 
uncertain project financing conditions. Neverthe less, we expect the Indian market to grow steadily by an 
average of 0.8 million tonnes annually with 2 to 5 commissionings per year.  
 
Background / market factors / legal framework 
India is the most populated country in the world. The 2021 decennial census has been postponed several 
times and the process is now expected to start in 2025. The population data will be announced by 2026. This 
is impacting the quality of all government statistical surveys, as they are still based on the 2011 census. India’s 
annual population growth has averaged 1.2% since 2011, down from 1.7% in the previous decade, as 
government data shows. 
Even though the share of people living in urban agglomerations has increased in recent years, India still is a 
very rural country, even when compared to other emerging countries such as China or Indonesia.  
The administration of an area in urban India is called urban local body (ULB). For the WtE market, the most 
important ULBs are the Municipal Cooperations (Nagar Nigam/Mahanagar Palika) that administer larger urban 
areas. 
For the future, all available prognoses expect a strong increase of waste amounts in India, as a result of the 
country’s growing population, its economic growth, and higher shares of collected MSW.  
There is no reliable data on waste amounts available, at least not at the national level. In its annual 
implementation report on waste legislation, the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) requires certain waste 
data from the federal states and union territories. According to the CPCB report for 2021/2022, total MSW 
amounts sum up to more than 150,847 tpd or 55.06 million tpy.  
However, it is noted that this information only includes the collected waste – and not the waste that was 
produced. In addition, the collected waste does not reflect those waste amounts that are collected by the 
informal sector due to their value as a secondary raw material, e.g. plastic or paper.

According to the report, 53.3 million tonnes (96.8%) of waste was collected. About 14.91 million tonnes 
(27.08%) of MSW was landfilled, while 25.90 million tonnes (47%) underwent some type of treatment. 
Moreover, 14.24 million tonnes (25.8%) were categorised as “unaccounted”. 
Similar figures are stated in a more recent report, the “State of India's Environment 2023” report by the research 
organization Centre for Science and Environment (CSE), according to which around 160,000 tpd of MSW is 
generated in India. 
The dominating form of MSW treatment is composting, with far more than 1,000 composting plants in India. 
The vast majority of them processes mixed waste, the composting of separately collected waste is very 
seldom. Other forms of treatment refer to anaerobic digestion and mixed waste treatment/RDF production.

The waste management directive “Solid Waste Management Rules, 2016” (SWM Rules, 2016) emphasises 
the obligations, planning, and reporting of state institutions. Amongst others, federal states are obliged to 
develop waste management strategies and the ULBs are required to work out waste management plans.  
The directive also strongly back thermal waste treatment. This refers to both “infrastructure creation” for WtE 
plants and “appropriate subsidy or incentives”. However, implementation remains difficult. The national level 
has a strong role in the legal fra mework for waste management, but the municipalities are responsible for 
implementing this legislation.  
As per the SWM Rules, 2016, all non -recyclable waste with a calorific value of at least 1,500 kcal/kg (6.3 
MJ/kg) should be used as RDF or processed towards RDF. Formally, this means a landfill ban for high calorific 
waste. However, Indian MSW in most cases does not reach 6.3 MJ/kg due to improper segregation practices. 
In addition, the enforcement of this rule is poor.

We do not know of further landfill reduction rules in individual states. On a national level no targets for material 
recycling have been agreed so far and we do not know of any federal state who has done this on a state-wide 
level either. 
In 2012, the Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (MNRE) recognized recovery of MSW as a renewable 
energy source. 
The Central Government’s program Swachh Bharat Abhiyan  (“Clean India Mission”), which started in 2014, 
was covering investments of 620 billion INR (8.4 billion EUR). Under this program,  the Ministry of Urban 
Development (MoUD) has received 53 proposals for the construction of WtE plants (electric generation 
capacity of 400 MW) from 22 Indian states. As of December 2023, according to the Central Government, the 
solid waste processing capacity had increased by around 38.6 million annual tonnes during first 8 years under 
the programme. 
There are various ministries dealing with WtE: 
- The Ministry of Environment, Forests and Climate Change (MoEFCC) is the policy -making body and 
concerned with determining policies and rules/directives, such as what types of waste can be used for 
WtE, calorific values, etc.  
- The Ministry of Urban Development (MOUD funds the projects. A certain amount is budgeted for 
projects and state governments may submit proposals to receive funding from this budget allocation.  
- The Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (MNRE) has its own scheme for WtE projects and its own 
budget to fund these projects.  
The main incentives for WtE facilities, however, result from feed -in tariffs (FITs) in single states. FITs are not 
mandatory, but most state Electricity Regulatory Commissions (ERCs) have adopted them. However, it is not 
possible to give details on all the funding schemes of the different federal states in this report. 
States are allowed to alter the FITs as per their requirements. The FITs of most WtE plants in India range 
between INR 6 to 8/kWh (EUR 0.067 to 0.089), which are determined in regard to the regulations of the state 
ERCs. Some states have introduced flexibl e tariffs that rely, amongst others, on the size and the capital 
expense of a project. The ERCs are responsible for both conventional and non -conventional (including WtE) 
sources.  
In October 2024, the Central Government approved the target sectors for India’s new Carbon Credit and 
Trading Scheme (CCTS), which is expected to be launched in the 2026-27 financial year. In phase I, the sector 
“waste handling and disposal” will be included in the CCTS. However, as of October 2024, it remains unclear 
whether WtE plants will also be covered by the scheme.

According to our data, 15 modern WtE plants in India use a technology identical or very similar to those plants

market.  
In addition to these 15 plants, we have identified another 11 facilities, for which WtE operation was reported in

Market development 
In general, we expect a promising future for the use of WtE in India. Some recent facilities, such as those in 
Delhi, made other ULBs and MCs realise that WtE can help phase out illegal dumpsites, provide proper 
disposal or treatment of the waste, and prev ent dumpsites or landfills from being overburdened. As a result, 
many ULBs would like to move towards establishing WtE facilities. 
However, there are a number of obstacles to the feasibility and efficacy of setting up WtE capacities in India, 
which is why most WtE projects are cancelled in the long run. 
Financing is the major issue, as municipalities usually do not charge waste fees from residents. Hence, there 
is no dedicated budget for the waste sector. As a result, most of the WtE projects are planned with zero gate 
fees and income should mainly be gen erated from the energy business, probably combined with grants or 
credits. Other options of power-production therefore diminish the viability of WtE.
