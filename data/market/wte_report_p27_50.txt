1 This year’s highlights and trends 
 
In this first chapter, we will highlight some recent developments on the Waste-to-Energy market. These are 
described in more detail in the following chapters. 
In the years to come, the global WtE market will continue to be led by Asia. China will remain the largest WtE 
market, but this growth will decline in the years to come. 
CO2 emissions and their pricing remain one of the most important issues in the WtE industry. However, 
implementation remains difficult. 
 
Chinese WtE market experiencing a major downturn 
The Chinese Waste-to-Energy market is experiencing a major downturn. Over the recent years already, new 
awardings have declined considerably. However, since 2023, this decline has even accelerated.  
The country’s populated eastern coastal provinces are already facing overcapacities and forced plant 
shutdowns. Therefore, in the future, we expect new commissionings to rather focus on the central and western 
regions. Moreover, in the medium and in the long term, greater recycling efforts will also lead to a decreasing 
demand for additional WtE plants. However, despite these developments, China will continue to lead the global 
market development, albeit at a significantly lower level compared to the previous years. China will still account 
for nearly half of all capacities worldwide to be built from 2024 to 2033 – in regard to tonnes per year. 
In the years to come, the global Waste-to-Energy market will continue to be led by the Asian countries. 
However, other promising markets, such as Thailand, Indonesia, India, and Malaysia, are not yet able to 
compensate for the declining Chinese market. In most of these countries, sufficient and sustained financial 
and regulatory support from the government continues to be lacking. In addition, many of these countries are 
struggling with political instability. 
In general, the European market will remain stable in the years to come. The long-lasting boom in new project 
awardings in the UK has come to an end. However, we still see market potential in countries such as Poland 
and the Czech Republic, as well as in markets like Italy and France.  
 
Discussions about carbon management 
At present, the potential of implementing carbon capture and storage (CCS) and carbon capture and utilisation 
(CCU) technology is widely being discussed in the WtE sector. CCS and CCU describes the technology used 
for the capture of CO2 at the emitter and the subsequent permanent storage or utilisation of the emissions. 
In recent years, several carbon capture projects have been announced at WtE plants in several countries, 
especially in Europe. The currently most promising CCS and CCU projects are located in the North Sea, and 
projects at geographically adjacent WtE facilities to utilise the storage sites are underway. A first CCU system 
is already operational at a WtE plant in the Netherlands, where the captured CO2 is used to supply local 
greenhouses.

price of more than 200 EUR/t of CO2 is considered as realistic. 
 
Market concentration in the EPC sector comes to a halt 
Since the publication of our last report, there have been no major changes among competitors in the WtE 
market. In recent years, the number of engineering, procurement and construction (EPC) providers in the 
European WtE market has decreased. Following a number of major takeovers in recent years, market 
concentration appears to have at least come to a halt. 
 
New market chapters, reflecting growing potential in the Middle East 
In general, we see a growing market potential in the Middle East, especially in economically stronger countries. 
For this year’s edition of our report, we have included the new market chapters Egypt, Kuwait, Qatar and Saudi 
Arabia. Specific information on efforts for developing a WtE market are often still lacking in these countries. In 
Saudi Arabia, for instance, incentives for the use of WtE have not yet been established. However, WtE gate 
fees are planned to be introduced in the Kingdom.

2 Plants worldwide 
 
Throughout the world, there are approximately 2,869 active thermal waste treatment plants by the end of 2023. 
Not all of them are generating energy and can therefore not be classified as WtE plants.  
Due to a special market structure with many small-scale plants, the Japanese WtE plant asset cannot be 
considered as representative for the global WtE market.  
 
Figure 1: Plants worldwide 
 
 
With about 200,000 annual tonnes, the average size of a European waste incineration plant is more than three 
times larger than a Japanese facility. However, newly installed large-scale plants in China, which increased 
the country’s average plant capacity to about 370,000 tpy, are slowly compensating for the low average 
capacities in Japan. Furthermore, the number of small-scale plants in Japan is decreasing. 
Considering the poor utilisation of the WtE plants in Japan of less than 50%, China by far processes the largest 
amounts of waste worldwide (based on the analysis of tonnes). 
The thermal waste treatment plants in the USA are even larger and have an average capacity of around 
400,000 tonnes of waste per year. The main reasons for this development are the energetic use of the plants 
and the higher pressure in terms of costs.

Grate combustion is the dominant incineration technology worldwide. It is not only preferred for untreated 
waste but in many cases also for processed waste, such as refuse-derived fuel (RDF) from MBT plants. The 
main reason for the dominance of grate combustion is its robustness in handling waste, which is a very 
heterogeneous fuel with many impurities. 
Fluidised bed incineration technology and especially gasification technology often need smaller particle sizes 
and processed waste, respectively. This is the reason why fluidised bed incineration technology usually 
accounts for a larger share amongst RDF power plants. The market share of other technologies, like plasma 
gasification, gasification or pyrolysis, remains very small. As per out database, between 2014 and 2024, only 
17 combustion lines using gasification or pyrolysis technology were commissioned worldwide.

2.1 
Europe 
For late 2023, we estimate 537 WtE plants to be operational in Europe with a combined capacity of 108.2 
million tonnes per year. 
The majority of capacities are located in France, the Benelux countries, Central Europe with Germany, 
Switzerland and Austria, followed by the UK & Ireland and Scandinavia. 
In regard to the population share, especially Scandinavia has high incineration capacities. Thermal waste 
treatment has a long tradition in countries such as Denmark or Sweden, due to, amongst other reasons, the 
major role WtE plants play for district heating schemes. Many of these countries incinerate more waste than 
they produce, which means that they import waste to different extents. 
The heat use improves the international competitiveness of the plants and creates a dependency on the 
continuous supply of waste as fuel. However, these countries also aim to increase recycling shares and reduce 
waste incineration in the future, for instance through including WtE in national or EU CO2 schemes. If waste 
imports decrease and WtE capacities are closed, the heat supply must be replaced.

2.2 
Asia 
The Asian WtE asset is dominated by Japan and China. However, these two markets differ considerably 
concerning their structure. 
Japan used to be the traditional – and for a long time the only – WtE market in Asia. Due to a very specific 
market structure in regard to legislation, domestic technologies and economic structure of the waste business, 
more than 1,500 thermal treatment plants have been constructed since the 1960s.  
Many of these plants use gasification and pyrolysis processes, due to, amongst others, the requirement of slag 
melting that was part of the Japanese legislation in the 1990s. Due to their peripheral location, many plants 
are very small and not in continuous operation; the overall utilisation of all plants amounts to less than 50%. 
Many of them were lacking any form of energy recovery.  
After the ash melting requirement was changed, the Japanese WtE asset increasingly shifts towards a 
structure similar to the rest of the world. Many small disposal plants are shut down and replaced by larger 
plants with an efficient energy recovery. This development reflects the trends towards more efficient, central 
plants with a higher energy yield and towards an improved recycling. The development in South Korea is a 
similar one.

2.3 
North America 
The WtE plant asset in the USA is on average 33 years old and the number of plants has been declining for 
many years. Most plants still result from a WtE boom in the 1980s and early 1990s. This boom was based on 
high overall energy prices and has not returned ever since. Only the most efficient plants are kept operational 
and hardly any new plants have been constructed or old boilers refurbished. As a result, more than 20 WtE 
plants were shut down in the past 20 years. 
In contrast to a strongly regulated waste management sector in Europe, North America is lacking such 
incentives.  
As in the US, the construction of WtE plants is resulting from market factors that are not directly influenced by 
the government, e.g., high energy prices, high disposal costs, lacking landfilling space or demands of the 
citizens.

3 Market development & forecast 
 
3.1 
Global market 
The global business for thermal waste treatment will continue to grow. We expect the number of plants to grow 
from currently 2,869 in late 2023 to about 3,104 by 20332. We expect the global treatment capacities to 
increase from around 576 million annual tonnes at the end of 2023 to about 726 million annual tonnes within 
the same period. This means that the international portfolio will increase by about 2.7% annually.  
 
Figure 15: Growth of capacities and market forecast until 2033 
 
 
The main reasons for this global growth remain: 
• 
The waste disposal pressure continues to increase, especially in economically growing countries due 
to increasing waste amounts and lacking landfilling space in urban areas and agglomerations. 
• 
In Europe, too, landfilling still must be restricted in many countries, especially in Eastern and Southern 
Europe, where countries have so far relied on MBT technology for waste treatment and will need 
additional incineration capacities for sorting residues.  
• 
A plant asset that continues to grow and gets older, which results in increasing investments in 
maintenance and renewal, for instance in countries like Germany or Switzerland.

Globally, the additional construction of capacities (in annual tonnes) will continue, albeit with a decreasing

In terms of capacity growth, Asia continues to dominate the market, again especially through the Chinese 
market. We still expect a growing capacity in China in the years to come. Coming from 29.8 million annual 
tonnes commissioned in 2023, we expect the annual capacity growth in China to gradually decrease to only 
3.3 million annual tonnes in 2033. The main reason for this development is that the Chinese WtE market has 
more or less collapsed, which has resulted significantly less new plant awardings since 2023. Nevertheless, 
China will still account for 48.7% of all capacities worldwide to be built from 2024 to 2033 – in regard to tonnes

India remains a promising WtE market. Until 2033, we assume 2 to 4 new WtE plants to start operations in 
India every year. The WtE potential is much higher in India. However, due to still lacking gate fees and 
bureaucratic hurdles, we do not expect this potential to be fully exploited.  
Other important Asian markets are Thailand, Indonesia, and Vietnam, that are also expected to realise more 
WtE projects in the future.

incineration and thus, if not accompanied by higher landfill taxes, could contradict efforts to stop landfilling. 
This might slow down or hinder the development of further WtE capacities. The regions where these actions 
are in place, however, are countries that already have a well-developed WtE market and are only building up 
little new capacity, e.g. Germany or the Netherlands.

In the long run, despite the slowdown in the Chinese market, prospects on the worldwide market for thermal 
waste treatment will remain very positive. A switch from landfilling to thermal waste treatment is usually 
irreversible. Only the increase of material recovery and a general prevention of waste generation results in a 
subsequent decline of waste for incineration. However, this potential is limited in most established economies 
and by far smaller than the opportunities that come along as a consequence of the global efforts towards the 
restriction of waste landfilling.

3.3 
Asia 
The development of thermal waste recovery in Asia will continue, but its intensity will slow down. 
In China, several areas in the populated eastern provinces are already facing overcapacities, at least in the 
large, populated eastern coastal provinces. We still expect a growing plant asset, yet again at a lower level 
compared to our forecast in the previous year.  
In 2023, we have tracked 90 new commissionings in China. For 2024, however, we have considered only 55 
commissionings in our project outlook. We predict about 86 million annual tonnes to be added in China 
between 2024 and 2033. 
Next to China, we also expect a growing WtE asset in countries such as Malaysia, Thailand, Indonesia, 
Vietnam and the Philippines, which have also increased efforts to implement WtE projects.  
We considered India as the potentially largest of the new developing WtE markets in Asia. However, as in all 
new WtE markets, many projects will fail in the medium and long term. These countries cannot be compared 
to the Chinese market, at least in the upcoming years, neither in terms of state subsidies nor in terms of a 
structured development. Nevertheless, we expect some successful projects.

3.4 
North America 
North America is characterised by a very old plant portfolio that is in many cases long overdue in terms of 
renewals. 
However, the right preconditions for such renewal measures have been lacking so far. Political framework 
conditions for thermal waste treatment remain difficult in the USA and Canada. There are no landfill bans and 
only few federal states or provinces have introduced some kind of a (low) landfill tax. 
As energy continues to be inexpensive, which is mainly the result of fracking, thermal waste recovery only 
features limited competitiveness. We anticipate a scenario where landmark decisions on their further 
operations have to be made for the ageing plants.  
These landmark decisions will be made in favour of the needed renewal of a WtE plant in agglomerations that 
lack landfill space. However, this would require a long-term disposal agreement with the respective 
municipalities. In the end, the operation of plants must rely more profoundly on disposal charges than it had in 
the past. 
Plants will be closed, as was the case in the past, in places that cannot assert sufficient disposal charges. In 
the USA this development has already begun. We assume further capacity cuts in the years to come.

3.6 
Rest of the world 
Globalisation on the WtE market is increasing. Up to now, the growth mainly focused on the European, Asian 
and North American markets. However, in recent years, first projects have been announced – and some even 
came to fruition – in South America, Africa, and Australia.  
In Australia, several projects were proposed next to the large agglomerations. The first projects are already 
under construction. Until 2033, we expect the development of about 2.5 million annual tonnes of thermal 
treatment capacity in this country. 
Brazil also has significant WtE potential. We expect the first WtE plant to be commissioned in 2027 with 
additional plants to follow in the years after, although the time schedule often remains uncertain. The still 
uncertain political situation continues to be the main risk for long-term investments in this new market.  
The same holds true for other South American countries that are facing waste management problems, such 
as Mexico, Colombia and Argentina. For instance in Mexico, backfilled landfills create the demand for thermal 
treatment capacity. However, policies supporting WtE capacity development are missing. 
The first project to be successfully realised in South America is expected to have a signal effect on the other 
countries, facing similar problems. This kind of signal effect could also be expected from the first project in 
Addis Ababa, Ethiopia, which became operational in 2019. In the past, several projects have been announced 
in other African countries, such as Nigeria, Ghana, Uganda and Kenya. 
Our market forecast until 2033 assumes 30 WtE plants to be realised apart from the major markets in Asia, 
Europe and North America.
